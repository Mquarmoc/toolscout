Grammarly AI Review 2026: More Than a Grammar Checker — But Not a Content Generator

Grammarly has quietly added powerful AI rewrite features on top of its best-in-class grammar engine. Here's our honest assessment of GrammarlyGO, what it does well, where it falls short, and whether Premium is worth the price.

What Is Grammarly in 2026?

Most people think of Grammarly as a grammar checker. That’s still accurate — it remains the best grammar and style editor available — but it undersells what the product has become. Over the past two years, Grammarly has added a full AI rewriting layer called GrammarlyGO that can generate text from prompts, adjust tone, expand bullet points into prose, condense long passages, and suggest structural improvements.

The result is a hybrid product: part real-time editing assistant, part AI writing tool. The editing side is world-class. The AI generation side is capable for short-form work but limited compared to dedicated tools. Understanding this distinction is essential to knowing whether Grammarly fits your workflow.

We’ve used Grammarly Premium for eight months as part of our regular content production process. Here’s the full picture.

The Grammar Engine: Still Best in Class

Let’s start where Grammarly’s reputation was built, because it’s still what makes the product worth considering.

Grammarly’s error detection is categorically better than the built-in checkers in Google Docs, Microsoft Word, or any general-purpose AI. In our testing, it caught:

  • Subject-verb agreement errors in complex sentences that Word missed entirely
  • Dangling modifiers that read naturally but were grammatically incorrect
  • Comma splices in long compound sentences
  • Ambiguous pronoun references (“the manager told the developer that he needed to fix it”)
  • Passive voice overuse (with the nuance to flag it as a style issue, not an error)
  • Word choice errors — homophones, near-synonyms used incorrectly, incorrect idioms

This matters. For professional writing — client proposals, press releases, technical documentation, job applications — catching these errors before they reach a reader is genuinely valuable. Grammarly’s detection rate is higher and its false positive rate is lower than any alternative we’ve used.

The Clarity score is particularly useful. Rather than just flagging individual errors, it gives your document a readability score and explains specific structural issues: sentences that are too long, transitions that don’t flow, word density that could be simplified. Learning to write toward Grammarly’s clarity feedback has made our team’s writing measurably better over time.

GrammarlyGO: The AI Rewriting Layer

GrammarlyGO, Grammarly’s AI feature, does several things:

Rephrase: Rewrite a selected passage in a different tone (formal, confident, casual, diplomatic). This works reliably for one to three sentences. For longer passages, tone consistency degrades.

Improve: Takes a selected passage and improves clarity, flow, and word choice without changing the meaning. The best use case: paste a rough draft sentence and get a cleaner version. This is where GrammarlyGO earns its keep.

Generate: Given a prompt, GrammarlyGO writes content — a paragraph, a list, a short email, an introduction. The quality here is the most variable. Short, structured output (email replies, social media captions, product bullet points) comes out usable. Longer generation (articles, detailed blog posts) produces generic content that needs substantial work.

Adjust tone: Select a passage and tell Grammarly to make it more professional, more friendly, more concise, or more persuasive. This is contextually aware — it reads the surrounding document and adjusts accordingly. More reliable than the “Rephrase” function for subtle tone shifts.

Where GrammarlyGO Genuinely Helps

After eight months of use, the AI features we actually use daily are narrow but real:

  1. Rewriting awkward sentences. When you know a sentence is wrong but can’t figure out why, the Improve function is faster than staring at it.
  2. Email tone adjustment. Making a mildly frustrated email sound neutral, or a neutral email sound warmer — GrammarlyGO handles this reliably.
  3. Expanding bullet points. “Turn this list of features into a paragraph” works well for short, factual expansions.
  4. Reply suggestions in Gmail. The “Reply” feature in Gmail analyzes the incoming email and suggests three response options in different tones. Genuinely useful for high-email-volume professionals.

Where GrammarlyGO Falls Short

Long-form content generation is not Grammarly’s strength. If you ask GrammarlyGO to write a 1,200-word blog post, you’ll get output that is technically correct but notably bland — the kind of content that reads like it was written by algorithm. The output lacks the perspective, structure, and specificity that makes writing worth reading.

For long-form content, use Claude, ChatGPT, or a dedicated tool like Writesonic. Grammarly is the tool you use to edit what those tools produce, not to replace them.

Where It Works: The Everywhere Factor

This is Grammarly’s most underrated advantage. It integrates into virtually every writing surface you use:

  • Browser extension (Chrome, Firefox, Edge, Safari): Works in Gmail, Google Docs, LinkedIn, Twitter/X, Notion, Confluence, Jira, Zendesk, and most web text inputs
  • Desktop app (Mac and Windows): Catches errors in any application — Word, Outlook, Slack desktop, VS Code
  • Google Docs integration: Native add-on with full Premium features
  • Microsoft Office integration: Word and Outlook add-in
  • Mobile keyboard (iOS and Android): Grammar checking in any mobile text field

The result is a writing assistant that follows you everywhere rather than requiring you to switch tabs. When you’re writing a Slack message, a client email, and a Google Doc in the same hour, Grammarly sees all of it and catches errors in each context.

No other tool in this review offers comparable coverage. ChatGPT and Claude require you to copy-paste into their interface. Jasper and Writesonic live in their own apps. Grammarly is ambient — it’s there when you need it, invisible when you don’t.

The free tier covers basic grammar and spelling correction — enough to catch typos and obvious errors. It does not include style suggestions, advanced clarity feedback, tone detection, plagiarism checking, or GrammarlyGO. If you write professionally, the free tier will feel like a constant invitation to upgrade.

Premium at $12/mo (billed annually) is the sweet spot. At $144/year, it’s cheaper than ChatGPT Plus ($240/year) and Claude Pro ($240/year), and it covers the core use case — making your writing better — without any of the prompting overhead.

The monthly rate at $30/mo is harder to justify. At that price, you’re comparing it directly to ChatGPT Plus and Claude Pro, both of which do more. If you can’t commit to a year, we’d suggest starting free and upgrading annually when you’re convinced.

Business at $15/seat/mo requires a minimum of 3 seats ($45/mo minimum). This is rough for solo professionals or two-person teams. The Business tier adds team style guides, tone goals, and admin controls — genuinely useful for larger content teams, but overkill below that threshold.

Grammarly vs. Alternatives

Grammarly vs. ChatGPT / Claude

These aren’t direct competitors. Grammarly edits your writing in real time wherever you work. ChatGPT and Claude generate and edit writing in their own interfaces. The comparison is like asking whether you should use a spell checker or a word processor — ideally, you use both.

If forced to choose one, the answer depends on your writing workflow. If you write in many different apps throughout the day, Grammarly’s ambient presence is more practically valuable. If most of your writing happens in one place and you’re willing to copy-paste, Claude or ChatGPT at the same price point can match or exceed Grammarly’s editing quality when explicitly prompted.

Grammarly vs. Hemingway App

Hemingway (free web tool, $20 one-time desktop app) focuses on readability: it flags complex sentences, passive voice, and adverbs with color coding. It’s a useful check for overly dense writing, but it’s purely a style tool — no grammar checking, no AI generation, no integrations. Use Hemingway for a periodic readability audit; use Grammarly for ongoing writing assistance.

Grammarly vs. ProWritingAid

ProWritingAid ($99/year or $399 lifetime) is a deep-editing tool popular with fiction writers. It has more detailed style reports than Grammarly — chapters of feedback on pacing, dialogue, consistency. But it’s slower, less integrated, and not designed for the quick, ambient correction flow that makes Grammarly valuable for business writing. If you’re writing a novel, ProWritingAid might be better. For everything else, Grammarly wins on practicality.

Real Usage Patterns

After eight months, here’s how GrammarlyGO and the editing features actually feature in our workflow:

Use it for:

  • All client-facing written communication (emails, proposals, reports)
  • Social media and marketing copy that needs to stay clean and on-brand
  • Technical documentation where precise language matters
  • Any writing that will be read by someone making a decision

Don’t use it for:

  • Generating long-form content (use Claude or Writesonic instead)
  • Creative writing where deliberate stylistic choices get flagged
  • Code comments (it triggers on syntax it misreads as prose errors)
  • SEO content workflows requiring keyword density controls

The Verdict: 8/10

Grammarly earns an 8 for being excellent at its core job — real-time, ambient grammar and style editing — while adding genuinely useful AI rewrite features that complement its editing strength. It’s not trying to be Claude, and it shouldn’t be. It’s trying to make all your writing better, wherever you write, without extra steps. At that specific mission, it’s the best product available.

The $12/mo annual rate is fair value for the quality of editing you get. The $30/mo monthly rate is harder to justify, but the annual commitment is a no-brainer for anyone who writes professionally.

We dock two points for:

  • AI generation quality that can’t match dedicated tools for long-form content
  • The steep monthly-vs-annual price cliff that penalizes cautious buyers

The bottom line: If you write professionally across multiple apps and communication channels, Grammarly Premium (annual) is worth it — it will catch errors that reach clients and collaborators and make a real difference in how your writing is received. If your writing mostly happens in one place and you already pay for Claude or ChatGPT, the marginal value is lower.
